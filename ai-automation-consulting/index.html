Automation vs. agent

Not sure which one you need?

Most of what marketing and ops teams call "we need AI" turns out to be plain automation: move data from A to B, format it, send it, on a schedule or a trigger. That's what this page covers, and it's usually cheaper and more reliable than an agent. You need AI agent consulting instead when the task requires judgment calls mid-workflow — deciding which of several tools to call next based on what it finds along the way. When in doubt, start with the Quick Scan — figuring out which one you actually need is exactly the call it's built to make.

FAQ

What teams ask before automating.

What is AI automation consulting?

Auditing your manual workflows, then building the automations that eliminate them — using tools you already pay for. Not every fix needs an AI agent; sometimes a well-built flow is the right, cheaper answer.

How much does it cost?

$8,000–$40,000 project-based depending on scope, plus an optional $3,000–$8,000/month retainer for ongoing sprints. Simple single-workflow automations land at the low end.

What's the difference between AI automation and RPA?

Traditional RPA replays fixed clicks and breaks when a screen changes. AI-assisted automation uses APIs plus language models for the parts that need judgment — reading text, classifying a lead — while keeping reliable steps as plain automation.

Do you sell new software?

No, by default. Most teams already have enough automation horsepower sitting unused in Microsoft 365, their CRM, and existing AI subscriptions. We only recommend new tooling when the existing stack genuinely can't do the job.

How fast can I see results?

A Quick Scan gives 3 recommendations in 30 minutes. A single-workflow automation typically ships in 2–4 weeks, tested alongside your manual process before switch-over.

What if my workflows are too messy to automate?

That's the norm, not the exception. Part of the audit is figuring out what's automatable now, what needs a small process fix first, and what should honestly stay manual.
