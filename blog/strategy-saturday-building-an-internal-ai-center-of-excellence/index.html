Strategy Saturday: Building an Internal AI Center of Excellence

Published April 18, 2026 — 4 min read

TL;DR: An AI Center of Excellence (CoE) is not a department — it's a cross-functional hub that sets standards, unblocks teams, and prevents AI fragmentation. The difference between scaling AI successfully and burning out your best engineers is whether you have operational governance, shared infrastructure, and a reuse-driven culture. This post covers the four pillars of building one that doesn't become a bottleneck.

---

1. Start With Governance, Not Tools

Most companies buy LLM platforms, hire an "AI lead," and hope engineers self-organize around best practices. That fails every time. You need governance _first_.

Your CoE's first job isn't to build AI features — it's to establish what counts as production-ready. Define:

  • Approval gates for which models are allowed (cost, latency, privacy constraints)
  • Eval standards that teams must pass before shipping (not vague hand-wavy testing)
  • Observability requirements (what logs, metrics, error handling are non-negotiable)
  • Escalation paths when a team's AI system starts drifting or degrading

This doesn't mean bottlenecking every experiment. It means setting a high bar for production and letting teams experiment freely in sandbox environments. The difference: your best engineer isn't debugging someone's LLM hallucination at 2 AM because "nobody said we couldn't use GPT-2 for financial summaries."

_Who owns this?_ A senior engineer + PM + security lead. Part-time at first. This is a cultural document more than a process.

---

2. Build Shared Infrastructure, Not Shared Ownership

Teams want autonomy. The CoE wants consistency. Reconcile this by providing infrastructure, not control.

Invest in:

  • LLM routing layer — abstracted interface that lets the CoE swap models/providers without breaking dependent teams (costs money upfront, saves chaos later)
  • Prompt versioning system — tracks which prompt generated which output, enables rollback, supports A/B testing
  • Eval harness — shared framework for running evals against your standards (saves each team from building this from scratch)
  • Observability dashboards — centralized view of model performance, cost per feature, latency p95s

Don't own the features. Own the plumbing. Teams stay fast; you stay aware.

The CoE team should be ~2-4 engineers who rotate through product teams. They're not gatekeepers — they're coaches embedded where problems happen.

---

3. Make Reuse Sticky With Patterns, Not Mandates

Every team wants to solve the same problems differently: prompt engineering, retrieval, multi-step reasoning, fallback handling. Your job is to make the "right" way the easiest way.

Document three things:

  1. Playbooks — step-by-step walkthroughs for common patterns (RAG, agent handoff, few-shot optimization). Real code examples. Real gotchas.
  2. Failure case library — "Here's what happens when you deploy GPT-4 with a 10K token context for real-time chat." Actual incidents + fixes.
  3. Decision frameworks — When should you fine-tune vs. prompt-tune? When should you add retrieval? When is local inference worth the latency? Teams should be able to self-serve these answers.

Pair this with a monthly knowledge-share where teams talk about what broke and what worked. Not a lecture. A round-table.

---

4. Measure Impact, Not Activity

A CoE that spends its time in meetings and Slack doesn't prove value. Measure:

  • Feature velocity per team — are product teams shipping faster because of your infrastructure? (proxy: features per sprint)
  • Model cost per feature — is the average team's LLM spend going down as they adopt better patterns?
  • Incident resolution time — when a model drifts, how long until it's detected and fixed?
  • Reuse rate — what % of new features use shared patterns/evals/infrastructure vs. inventing new ones?
  • Time to production — how long from "we want an AI feature" to deployed and monitored?

Pick two of these per quarter. Make them visible. Report monthly to leadership. This is how you prove the CoE isn't overhead.

---

FAQ

Q: Should the CoE control which models teams use?

A: No. The CoE sets minimum standards (cost, latency, privacy, eval bars). Teams pick their model within those constraints. If a team wants Claude + a team wants GPT-4, let them run the evals and prove their choice. Then log what they picked — you'll spot patterns.

Q: How do I staff it without poaching my best engineers?

A: Rotation model. An engineer spends 3 months embedded with the CoE, then returns to product. They bring knowledge back. The CoE brings product reality back. Avoid career-track people who live there permanently — that makes it a silo.

Q: What's the minimum viable CoE?

A: One senior engineer + one PM, part-time. Their job: governance + one shared tool (usually the eval harness). Expand from there. Don't over-hire at the start.

Q: How do I prevent it from becoming a bottleneck?

A: Automate approvals. If a team's change passes the governance gates, it ships. No human sign-off needed. The CoE reviews async once it's live.
